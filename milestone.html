What We Have Accomplished

We have a working CPU-based watercolor simulation running in Godot at 64×64. All five physics stages from the proposal are implemented and running together each frame:

  • Water displacement. Gravity, surface tension, and spreading force move water around. We use a 4-directional redistribution scheme to avoid the checkerboard pattern we ran into with the David Small paper formulation.
  • Inertia tracking. Water flow has momentum — it speeds up when continuing in the same direction and slows down when reversing. We have two tunable parameters for this that can be adjusted live while the sim runs.
  • Evaporation, diffusion, and deposition. Water dries over time, wet pigment spreads into neighboring wet areas, and as the water evaporates the pigment sticks to the paper permanently.

We also have a watercolor brush with Wacom pressure support, a pencil, an eraser, a color picker, and canvas tilt controls (W/A/S/D) that apply gravity in any direction to produce dripping.

Preliminary Results

The physics all work together. You can paint a stroke, tilt the canvas, and watch the water carry pigment downhill. Wet strokes bleed into each other. The paint gradually dries and leaves a stain. At 64×64 it runs at interactive frame rates, which is what we needed to validate the physics before moving to the GPU.

The CPU version is too slow for anything larger than that, which is expected. The GPU port is the next big step.

Progress Relative to Plan

We are roughly on track. Week 1 was supposed to get the CPU prototype working with water displacement and a basic brush, and we have done that and more — all five physics stages are in and the brush system is more complete than planned. The GPU port has not started yet, which is the main thing we need to get moving on.
